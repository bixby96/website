Introduction

With the recent start of the college basketball season, I thought it would be interesting to take a look at how style of play varies between conferences and explore how the game has changed over time. Using yearly data, a site for advanced college basketball advanced stats, I examined the state of college hoops.

Pace of Play

To begin, let’s take a look at how tempo has varied over time. Kenpom defines tempo as a team’s total possessions per 40 minutes (the length of a game minus overtime) adjusted for opponent. So, in general, the greater the tempo, the faster pace a team plays at. The plot below maps how tempo has evolved over time with the red dot representing each year’s mean.

## Warning: New theme missing the following elements: panel.grid, plot.tag,
## plot.tag.position

Most teams had slowly but surely been reducing the game’s overall pace; tempo was trending downwards right until the 2016 season. So what happened in 2016? The NCAA reduced the shot clock from 35 seconds to 30 and looks to have made an immediate impact on the way teams play. The average team possessions jumped from 64.4 in 2015 to 68.2 in 2016, the highest since 2002. That’s an increase of about 8 more total possessions per game! Next, let’s break down style of play on the conference level.

Conference Clustering

Each school competes in one of 32 different conferences which are determined primarily by geography and school size. Not all conferences are created equally, however. While open to debate, I think each conference can be classified as belonging to one of three categories. At the top of the food chain, “Power 6” conferences (ACC, Big 10, Big 12, Big East, Pac-12, SEC) recruit the top talent year in, year out and are the most competititve each year. The rest of the confereces could be considered “mid-major”, but I’ll make a distinction to include one more category: “high-major”. It wouldn’t be unusual for high-major conferences (American, Atlantic 10, Mountain West) to send more than one team each year to the NCAA tournament, but they are not as consistently competitive from top to bottom as the stronger Power Six schools.

cluster.data <- ncaa %>%

  filter(year > 2010) %>%
